01 / About

A biologist who builds, at the bench and in code.

My work follows one throughline: understand the rules of cell fate well enough to rewrite them. I started at the mechanism level — how chromatin marks like H3K4me3 shape gene regulation and replication. To read those rules genome-wide, I built long-read (nanopore) single-molecule approaches paired with machine learning to map replication dynamics directly.

Now I work on the writing problem — reprogramming aged and dysfunctional cell states at scale — using multiplexed CRISPR-Cas13d perturbation and single-cell readouts.

What ties it together is platform-building. I care about generating the high-quality, high-dimensional perturbation data that better models need, and closing the loop between wet-lab experiments and computation fast enough that each round sharpens the next.

I'm equally at home at the bench and in Python, and I increasingly build agentic AI systems to plan experiments, ground hypotheses in the literature, and prioritize targets.

02 / Research vision

Predictive programming of cell fate.

Biology still can't reliably tell a cell what to become. I'm working to change that — moving from reading the mechanism to writing the state, in four stages.

1
Read the mechanism

Cell fate is written in chromatin

My early work dissected epigenetic control — H3K4me3 and chromatin regulation — at the level of molecular mechanism. The foundation for everything downstream.

2
Measure the rules at scale

Reading replication at native resolution

I built long-read, single-molecule (nanopore) methods with machine learning to map genome-wide replication dynamics directly, rather than inferring them — reading one of the most fundamental processes of life as it happens.

3
Rewrite cell state

Safer rejuvenation, understood systematically

Can we reprogram aged cell fate safely? I work on reprogramming at scale and understanding it systematically — functional-genomic screens for the factors that restore youthful, functional states without losing safety.

4
Build the platform

Data at the scale better models need

The bottleneck isn't ideas — it's data. I build systems that produce high-plex perturbation data at the quality and scale that training genuinely predictive models requires, filling the gap between screens and the foundation models meant to learn from them.

03 / Platforms & tools

What I build, framed as capabilities.

Perturbation at scale

Multiplexed Cas13d screens

Design and deployment of MEGA-Cas13d arrays for combinatorial RNA-level perturbation in primary human cells, with single-cell and surface-proteomic readouts engineered to be analysis-ready.

Wet ↔ dry loop

End-to-end Perturb-seq pipelines

Reproducible single-cell analysis (scanpy, pertpy, pyDESeq2, Mixscape, scVI) built so results feed computation — and the next experiment — fast.

Hypothesis engine

AI-driven target selection

Agentic pipelines combining deterministic scoring, automated literature grounding, and orchestration to prioritize gene combinations — fusing experimental judgment with computational prediction.

Direct measurement

Long-read + ML methods

Nanopore single-molecule assays paired with machine learning to quantify genome dynamics directly, at single-molecule resolution.

04 / AI × Science

I work AI-natively.

Beyond running models on my data, I design and build multi-agent systems — for experimental planning, protocol development, literature grounding, and data interpretation. I treat these tools critically: probing where they fail, turning those failures into concrete evaluations, and iterating. The scientists who move biology fastest will be the ones who can both run the assay and build the agent that plans it.
